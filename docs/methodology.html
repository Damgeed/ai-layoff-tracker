Methodology

How we collect, classify, and verify every entry. This page protects the integrity of the project.

Mission

The AI Layoff Tracker is a public research database documenting workforce reductions where artificial intelligence or automation was explicitly cited as a contributing factor. We do not editorialize. We do not advocate. We record, classify, and cite.

"These are the documented cases. Read the sources and decide."

Inclusion Criteria

An entry is added to the database only when all of the following conditions are met:

  1. Public source required. The workforce reduction must be documented in at least one publicly accessible source — a news article, company press release, SEC filing, earnings call transcript, or official regulatory document.
  2. Workforce reduction documented. There must be a specific, identifiable reduction in headcount (layoffs, hiring freezes that target specific roles, contractor reductions, or department eliminations).
  3. AI explicitly referenced. The company or a credible third-party source must explicitly cite artificial intelligence, automation, or machine learning as a contributing factor to the workforce reduction.
  4. Verifiable date. The announcement or implementation must have a specific date (at minimum, month and year).

Exclusion Criteria

The following are not included in the database:

  • General economic layoffs — workforce reductions attributed solely to market conditions, cost-cutting, or restructuring without AI being mentioned.
  • Rumors or anonymous claims — unverified reports, social media speculation, or anonymous sources without corroboration.
  • Speculative projections — studies predicting future AI-related job losses without specific, documented company actions.
  • Normal turnover — routine attrition, retirement, or voluntary departures not linked to AI.
  • Offshoring without AI — jobs moved to other countries for cost reasons without AI being a stated factor.

Classification System

Every entry is assigned to one of four tiers. This distinction is the foundation of the database's credibility. Journalists and researchers can filter by tier to match their editorial standards.

TierClassificationDefinitionConfidence Required
Tier 1 Direct AI Replacement Company explicitly states AI systems replaced specific human work, resulting in workforce reduction. 80+
Tier 2 AI-Driven Restructuring Company cites AI efficiency or automation as a major factor in a restructuring that included layoffs. 70+
Tier 3 AI Reallocation Jobs cut while the company simultaneously announced increased investment in AI, with resources redirected toward AI priorities. 70+
Tier 4 Market Disruption Company lost revenue or market position because customers switched to AI-powered alternatives, leading to workforce reductions. 80+

Classification Examples

Klarna — Tier 1 · Direct AI Replacement

Klarna CEO stated the company reduced its workforce from 5,000 to 3,000 because "we can do the same amount of work with 2,000 employees that we previously did with 5,000 people," citing AI-powered customer service as the enabling technology. Confidence: 95/100.

IBM — Tier 2 · AI-Driven Restructuring

IBM CEO Arvind Krishna announced a hiring pause for approximately 7,800 back-office roles, stating "I could easily see 30 percent of [back-office roles] getting replaced by AI and automation over a five-year period." AI was cited as the primary driver of workforce strategy change. Confidence: 85/100.

Google — Tier 3 · AI Reallocation

Google laid off 12,000 employees while CEO Sundar Pichai stated the company needed to "redirect resources toward AI priorities." Jobs were cut across multiple departments as investment shifted to AI development. Confidence: 75/100.

Chegg — Tier 4 · Market Disruption

Chegg laid off 441 employees (20% of workforce) after ChatGPT's launch caused a "significant spike in student interest" in free AI tools, directly reducing demand for Chegg's homework help services. Confidence: 90/100.

Confidence Scores

Each entry receives a confidence score from 0 to 100, reflecting the strength and directness of the evidence linking AI to the workforce reduction.

Score RangeRatingCriteria
90–100 HighCEO or official company statement directly and unambiguously attributes workforce reduction to AI. Multiple corroborating sources.
70–89 MediumCompany statement cites AI as a factor, but with less direct causal language. Or strong third-party reporting with named sources.
50–69 LowAI mentioned in context of broader restructuring. Single source. Inferred connection.
Below 50InsufficientEntry does not meet our threshold for publication. May be held in review queue.

Evidence Types

Entries are tagged with one or more evidence types that describe the nature of the source material:

  • CEO_STATEMENT — Public statement by the company's CEO or equivalent executive.
  • COMPANY_STATEMENT — Official company press release, blog post, or spokesperson statement.
  • EARNINGS_REPORT — Quarterly or annual earnings filing, investor presentation, or shareholder letter.
  • SEC_FILING — Filing with the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, etc.).
  • MEDIA_REPORT — Verified reporting by a recognized news organization.
  • REGULATORY_FILING — Filing with government agencies outside the U.S. (WARN Act notices, EU collective redundancy notifications, etc.).
  • TRAFFIC_ANALYSIS — Third-party web/app traffic data showing measurable decline linked to AI competition.

Verification Process

  1. Source collection. Entry is submitted or identified through monitoring of news, company announcements, and public filings.
  2. Primary source verification. The original source (press release, SEC filing, earnings call transcript) is located and reviewed.
  3. Classification assessment. Based on the directness of the AI attribution, the entry is classified into one of four tiers.
  4. Cross-reference. At least one additional source is located to corroborate the primary source (where available).
  5. Confidence scoring. A score is assigned based on evidence strength, source quality, and clarity of AI attribution.
  6. Publication. Entry is added to the database with full source links and metadata.

Data Sources

Our data is drawn from verified public sources including but not limited to:

  • Company press releases and official blogs
  • SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K)
  • Earnings call transcripts
  • Verified media reporting (Reuters, Bloomberg, BBC, CNBC, The Verge, Ars Technica, etc.)
  • Government regulatory filings (WARN Act notices, EU directives)
  • Investor presentations and shareholder letters

We maintain a domain whitelist of trusted sources. Submissions referencing sources outside this whitelist are held for manual review.

Corrections Policy

We are committed to accuracy. If you believe an entry contains an error:

  1. Email corrections@ailayofftracker.com with the entry ID, the specific error, and supporting sources.
  2. Corrections are reviewed within 72 hours.
  3. Accepted corrections are published with a changelog entry documenting what was changed, when, and why.
  4. All corrections are logged in our public changelog.

Transparency Statement

  • No AI is used to classify entries. Every classification decision is made by human review.
  • No automated scraping populates the database. Every entry is manually reviewed before publication.
  • No funding from companies tracked in this database. This project is independently maintained.
  • No paywall. All data is freely available in JSON, CSV, and RSS formats.
  • Full source attribution. Every entry includes links to original sources.
  • Dataset versioning. Each significant update is versioned for citability.
